Bands

There are a limited number of useful radio frequencies available. The Federal Communications Commission or FCC has the job or regulating and partitioning the various segments or bands of frequencies for specific uses. There are large bands such as HF, VHF, UHF and so on, that describe very loosely where a radio frequency lies. Then there are smaller bands within these larger bands that are dedicated for specific uses. Some of these are the AM and FM broadcast bands, ISM(Industrial, Scientific and Medical) bands and the various Amateur Radio bands. Within a band there are usually specific frequencies that are dedicated to very specific use. For example, 467.6125mhz is assigned as channel 10 on the GMRS/FRS(General Mobile Radio Service/Family Radio Service) band, is limited to an output of 0.5 watts and a bandwidth of 11khz. If you listened to this frequency, there’s a good chance you would hear families communicating while camping or on a road trip. You would also likely hear one of the younger family members repeatedly sending obnoxious call tones to each other.

GeoRF is a convenient way to visualize how all of these bands and frequencies fit into the greater spectrum of RF frequencies. Enter a search for a frequency or a station you know about, and it will show up on the frequency display along with other common frequencies nearby. Zoom out and you can see the result in relation to larger bands of frequencies. It’s enlightening to see how much is going on.
