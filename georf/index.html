The Electro-magnetic spectrum

Everywhere you go, there are countless radio waves, whizzing by, carrying payloads of information: AM and FM broadcasts, emergency communications or just families using hand held radios. Wouldn’t you like to see where all the interesting things are happening on the radio waves in your area? GeoRF has all the information you need to become familiar with the EM spectrum and the tools to find where you can find interesting things and perhaps even listen in.

Radios have both gotten smaller and more capable over time. Something like this would have a weak signal and very limited frequency range.
Electromagnetic energy is emitted as waves that radiate outward from their source like a sphere. Imagine the wave in three dimensions as a white ping pong ball with a radius that grows bigger and bigger at the speed of light. Now imagine a new black ball that grows in the same way from the same point. Repeat this over and over and at an extremely high frequency and it’s a good way to imagine how electromagnetic waves work. The white balls would represent peaks in the intensity of the electromagnetic energy and the black balls represent that same energy, but of the opposite polarity. Opposite polarity means the waves exert influence in an opposite direction. The speed at which successive waves are emitted determine their frequency. Frequency might sound familiar; When you listen to the radio, you tune in a frequency. Radio frequencies are an example of EM waves. There are many other ranges or bands of EM frequencies: Radio, x-ray, gamma-rays and light are all names for bands of EM wave frequencies. The radio waves that allow you to listen to the radio and the light from the sun are the same phenomenon.
